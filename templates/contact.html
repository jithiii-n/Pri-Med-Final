CKKS Encryption in Encrypted CNN

The CKKS (Cheon-Kim-Kim-Song) scheme is a leveled homomorphic encryption scheme that supports approximate arithmetic on encrypted data. It allows the encryption of real-valued data, making it an ideal fit for machine learning tasks such as training neural networks on encrypted data. CKKS allows operations such as addition, multiplication, and rotation on encrypted data, with the ability to support multiple levels of precision, which is crucial for applications in privacy-preserving machine learning.

In our project, CKKS encryption is leveraged to train Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) on encrypted data without decrypting it. The main challenge in encrypted machine learning is that the encrypted data is represented as ciphertext, which can only be manipulated through specific homomorphic operations. The CKKS scheme allows CNNs to perform convolutions, activations, and pooling operations while keeping the data encrypted, ensuring privacy and security throughout the training process.

Training Methodology

The training methodology for our Encrypted CNN leverages the CKKS scheme to securely process and train on encrypted datasets. The entire training pipeline is designed to operate within the encrypted domain, ensuring that sensitive data never needs to be decrypted. Below is an overview of our training methodology:

  • Data Encryption: The training data is first encrypted using CKKS encryption before being fed into the CNN model. This ensures that sensitive information, such as images or other personal data, remains confidential.
  • Forward Propagation: During the forward pass, each layer of the CNN performs operations such as convolution and activation on the encrypted data. The CKKS scheme supports approximate computations, which is essential for maintaining the accuracy of the network while preserving privacy.
  • Backpropagation: The backpropagation process updates the model parameters (weights) using the encrypted gradients, enabling the model to learn from the encrypted data without exposing it.
  • Decryption: After training, the final model can be decrypted to extract the learned parameters, but the training data itself remains encrypted throughout the process.
